What it is

A legal monopoly over your brand's identity.

A trademark is any distinctive sign – a name, logo, tagline, shape, colour combination or even a sound – that identifies the source of goods or services. Registration under the Trade Marks Act, 1999 gives you the exclusive right to use it in the class of goods or services you apply for, and to stop others from using a confusingly similar mark.

India follows the Nice Classification, which divides all goods and services into 45 classes – classes 1 to 34 cover goods and classes 35 to 45 cover services. A single application filed in Form TM-A covers one applicant, one mark and one class; a multi-class filing is possible but treated internally as separate applications. Once registered the mark is valid for 10 years and renewable indefinitely.

Wordmark

Protects the brand name or tagline in plain characters, independent of font or colour.

Device mark / logo

Protects a specific graphic, symbol or stylised artwork.

Composite mark

Combination of word and device – stronger against lookalikes than either alone.

Non-traditional

Shape, 3D, colour combination, sound – possible but higher distinctiveness threshold.

Our process

From search to certificate.

1

Public search & conflict check

We run a public search on the Trade Marks Registry database for identical and phonetically similar marks in the relevant class, so you do not waste months on a mark that will be objected to.

2

Class selection & specification

We map your business to the right Nice class(es) and draft a tight goods / services specification – neither too narrow to miss real uses nor too broad to attract objections.

3

Form TM-A filing

We file TM-A online with the mark image, user details and TM-48 POA. The application gets an application number and you can start using the "TM" symbol immediately.

4

Examination & objection response

The Registry issues an examination report, often raising objections under sections 9 or 11. We draft a legal reply within 30 days and, if required, attend the show-cause hearing.

5

Journal publication

On acceptance the mark is published in the Trade Marks Journal. Third parties have four months to file an opposition. We monitor the journal and address any opposition that comes in.

6

Registration certificate

If the journal period passes without opposition – or opposition is defeated – the Registry issues the e-certificate. You can now use the ® symbol, and the mark is valid for 10 years.

Frequently asked

Trademark questions founders ask.

What is the difference between TM and R?

add

The "TM" symbol can be used the moment an application is filed – it signals that you are claiming rights over the mark and that it is under examination. The "®" symbol can only be used after the Registry issues the Registration Certificate. Using ® before registration is itself an offence under the Trade Marks Act, so clients commonly use TM during the entire application journey and switch to ® only on the day the certificate arrives.

The Registry has raised an objection – is my mark dead?

add

Not at all. Objections under section 9 (absolute grounds – lack of distinctiveness, descriptive mark) and section 11 (relative grounds – similarity to an existing mark) are very common, especially for coined and common-word brands. The applicant has 30 days to file a written reply with legal arguments, user evidence and affidavits. Many objections are overcome at this stage itself, and the remaining ones are cleared at a show-cause hearing.

How long does the whole process take?

add

If the application sails through without objection or opposition, the certificate can come in about 12 to 18 months. If there are objections or hearings, it can take longer. The good news is that protection is retrospective to the date of application, and you can start using the "TM" mark and enforcing your rights from day one. The registration, when it comes, is valid for 10 years and indefinitely renewable.

Do I need an Indian registration to protect my brand abroad?

add

Trademarks are territorial – an Indian registration protects you only in India. For international protection you have two routes: filing directly in each country, or using the Madrid Protocol via WIPO, which lets you designate multiple member countries from a single application based on your Indian base application or registration. The Madrid route is usually cheaper and faster for brands targeting several geographies.

Should I file the wordmark, the logo, or both?

add

A wordmark is the strongest form of protection because it protects the brand name regardless of how it is written or styled. A logo protects only that particular artwork – if you rebrand, the old logo mark loses most of its value. Ideally we advise a wordmark first and a separate device mark for the logo once the brand matures. Composite mark applications (word + device together) are cheaper but weaker if the two elements drift apart later.
